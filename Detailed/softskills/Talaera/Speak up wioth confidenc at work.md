Participating in meetings and discussions in English can be difficult for non-native speakers. 
In this lesson, you'll learn strategies for prepare for and participate in conversations. \
No more sitting silently because you're too nervous to talk!



Preparation

Before you have an important meeting or conversation, do these three things:

Organize your thoughts: Outline what you want to say and how you'll say it. Write down the key points you want to discuss. Practice saying them aloud.
Find any vocabulary you may need: If you're unsure about certain words, look them up and understand their context.
Practice pronunciation: Familiarize yourself with difficult words by practicing them aloud. Repeat difficult words like "statistics," "entrepreneurship," or "sustainability."

EASY 🙂
I think that the best way to boost sales is by focusing on our online advertising.
In my opinion, our next project should prioritize sustainability.
To be honest, I believe our current marketing strategy needs an overhaul.
I feel that our team could benefit from more regular check-ins and communication.

PRO 😎
I'm fairly confident that we can cut costs by optimizing our supply chain.
If you ask me, switching to remote work on Fridays could improve overall employee satisfaction.
My initial reaction to the proposal is to conduct a detailed risk assessment first.
Off the top of my head, I suggest we allocate at least 10% of our budget to employee training.
After much thought, I feel that we need to invest more in customer support to enhance client relationships.
To my mind, our current approach to product development might be too rushed, and we should consider a more thorough analysis.




Listen to participants actively and address them
A great way to be heard in meetings is actually listening to others. Address people and ask for their opinion. If you are face to face, make eye contact. This way, people will be more willing to respect your ideas and listen to you.

Sofia, what do you think about…?
Oliver, how do you feel about…?
Martin, what’s your opinion on…?
Marie, what are your views on…?
Jonas, do you have any particular thoughts on…?
Emma, I’d love to hear your thoughts.
James, what’s your take on…?





Building confidence

We're almost there! The last section in this lesson is about helping you feel more confident to speak up at work. Here are some important tips:

Practice. Practice makes perfect. Don't worry so much about being perfect. What matters is that you feel comfortable with your message and that others can understand it. 
Seek feedback. Ask native speakers or colleagues for feedback. If you can, work 1:1 with a teacher and go over the most common topics you need to discuss at work.
Learn some idioms. Understanding and using idioms can make your language more natural and -more importantly- can help you become more confident.

Quick reminder!
An idiom is a phrase whose meaning isn't obvious from the individual words. For example, "break a leg" means "good luck," not actually breaking a leg!

Learning idioms can boost your confidence in speaking English at work because it helps you sound more natural. You'll be able to understand casual expressions and connect better with native speakers, making conversations more engaging and enjoyable. It's like knowing the secret handshake in the world of English!





You took the words right out of my mouth!
Definition: You said exactly what I was thinking.
Example: Yes, that’s exactly what I was about to say. You took the words right out of my mouth.
I'm all ears 
Definition: I am listening attentively and eager to hear what you have to say.
Example: Eve, you wanted to tell me about the meeting with the client. I'm all ears.
Play the devil's advocate
Definition: Express an opinion that you may not agree with but which is very different from what other people have been saying, in order to make the argument more interesting.
Example: I agree with you, but I'll play devil's advocate so that we can cover all the possibilities.
Speak my mind
Definition: Say what you think.
Example: Even if I'm shy about speaking in English, I always try to speak my mind.
